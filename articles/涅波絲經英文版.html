Prologue: The Revelation of the Greatest Sin

One afternoon, he stood where the waves kissed the sand like a spirit in pain, where the sea-wind and the sorrow were half in cast wails but he came not for joy, only for oblivion—fled from dreams those shadows of yesteryear!

And lo! And then, as he turned to take the long, lonely way home, there met his gaze a most curious sign:

"Hast thou eaten thy burger yet today?"

He lifted his eyes and read the words, but he stared a long, long while, as if he were interpreting a language not of his world. The letters curled like a prophet’s script, complete and round, and were hewn by hand across a radiant expanse of deep cerulean — a shade not of this world, but called into existence to wrench him from his despair.

Lost in contemplation and numbed by the crush, onward he pushed—

And there, under that sign, was a door:

An old, ancient, ajar wooden gate with a bronze sign above it that reads this name:

The Church of Nebo.

An invisible power now laid hold on his limbs and impelled him inward. Its wood creaking and caked dust exhaling from between its crevices, the door yawned open — and he entered.

What world was this?

A cathedral impossible to draw by hand or dream — walls of glass, a kaleidoscope divine; a ceiling vast, as constellations bound in time. And at its center: a towered and reeling presence, a statue tall and shuddering, hands clasped in reverent pose, brow aglow from holy light.

The face was mercy, the pose judgment.

It was She. Neboskey.

He stood as struck. Yea, struck to the marrow. Some strange sacredness had taken hold of him and squeezed from the depths a shiver — a sensation he had not known for years.

And he whispered — nothing but his soul heard it:

What is this tingling... this gentle loosening of my Worst Sin?

And finally a voice — a man’s, lined by linen and awe:

“She sends for thee as well. Come, stranger. Shed your Most Sin, and come along with us, the faithful of Nebo.”

“Then... so be it,” he said, as if the words had been years in waiting.

“Let me be enrolled with you.”

So it was, the rite began.

The Priestess loomed over him in a room as dark as the beginning of time, under the hidden stars of the dome.

And then the foot — her sacred sole over his chest.

And then the deluge — holy water, a flood from on high, stifling but holy.

For in the death of the flesh, the soul was able to breathe again.

So were they born, the rite was accomplished – by submission, by silence, by smothering – the Most Sin was purged.

And afterward, he took to himself a new name in faith:

Geesman.

So begins the tale.

The story of rise, of challenges, of truths uninvited.

Chapter One: The Genesis of Geesman's Faith

On a silent, stifling morning, the hallowed hall filled with the rarest scent — no, not incense but burgers, warm and delicious.

And lo! She drew nigh, majestic, not as mortals step, but as a nightmare sheathed in secretest mantle.

The color of her hair was ash and argent twisted together, her garb a black-and-white dance of pattern, her stockings and the color of the skies on a day kissed by some unheard grace.

Instantly, a pang of memory struck him in the gut. Where had he seen her face? Ah! The statue, rehearsed in stone—can it be? Was this the High Priestess, buried in a legend, whom men name the Highest Hierophant?

Then she spoke, as softly she replied:

"Hail, my faithful. Welcome to the Church of Nebo. I am thy Hierophant... Neboskey."

And hearing thus, he fell. His knees buckled, not of his volition, but wrenched by the force of awe; worship sprang up not as something he did but as something done to this man from inside.

She spake no more. There was only silence, and from that silence a smile emerged — soft, unfathomable, divine.

Chapter Two: The Morning of Geesman

As the world traded in the day for the night, there in the twilight of dawn, the bell of Nebo Church awakened the whole of its heavenly followers.

And he, even as they, prayed not from lip, but from heart, and said within himself:

"Nebo, Nebo… O mighty Hierarch of the Highest Flame, I thank thee for thine embrace. Lo, I stand here before thee, nekked and bare of my sin; I cast out and am all white and beyond thee and above thee."

Hand in hand they strolled, not to an altar or to a holy writ, for the matter-whereunto was a hall— but not a hall, instead a sanctum, a temple made to look as a fast-food den.

This, they knew, was no ordinary restaurant, but the Common Table of the Devout.

Now, each soul is to wash their flesh in the consecrated water, singing this song as they wash:

"With holy water I will bathe and It shall cleanse my soul."

So cleansed they returned to their places and sat waiting for their heavenly meal: a dish of Geesman and a cup of tea, not common leaves, commonplace steeps of hours.

For legend tells how the soup is conceived of holy water, and the tea, ah, it steeps in sanctity — it steeps for seven-thousand seven-hundred and forty-nine hours in Nebo's favor.

By the time the meal was finished, nothing remained of the burger crumb of the sacred offering.

Yet the godly rose and sized, their robes a rustling like unto the whispering of angels, and they moved as one to the Great Church, there to chant the Doctrine, eternal and ever-burning…
